Spaghetti mit Spinat

by Sophie Hunger

Sophie Hunger is one of these true cosmopolitans and the variety of her music is astonishing. She is Swiss, but also grew up in Germany and England, she composes and sings in five languages (German, French, English, Swiss Italian and Swiss German), she plays guitar, blues harp and piano and her style ranges from folk, jazz to electronic music. She called her style once „minimalistic electronic folk music“. After she took a little break she released her 5th album „Supermoon“ in 2015. It is very characteristic for her unique variety of styles and languages.

My favorite song of hers, however, is a bit brighter and more melodic than most of her work and truly a hidden gem. It was only released as a bonus track on a deluxe version of the mentioned album „Supermoon“. It is not often available (unfortunatly not on Spotify), but you can enjoy it on YouTube. There, it comes with a really cool and cinematic music video that perfectly complements the lyrics.

Lyrics

„Du hast gut Lachen“ is a German expression, that cannot be translated literally like Google would do it („you have a good laugh“). It rather means, that „you“ are in a good position and can „very well laugh about it“.
Du bist wie ein Versprechen

Aber draußen dort beim Gästebuch
7) But outside, there at the guest book
Ja, da fällt dir leider überhaupt gar nichts ein
8) well there, unfortunaltly absolutely nothing comes to your mind
The „Rhein“ is Germanys biggest river and it is often mythologically charged. However, the meaning of that line is up for interpretation. Of course there are no coral reefs in the Rhine… The „Ja“ in line 8 has no specific meaning. It’s like a „yeah“ or a „well“ in spoken English and gives a little more emphasis on the the da/there.

„klitzeklein“ is the colloquial belittlement of „klein“. Instead of tiny you could also say teeny or itty-bitty.

„nehme … ein“ (or „einnehmen“) is a rather formal way of saying „to have something to eat/dinner“. „Abendbrot“ is not quite the same as dinner, which would be „Abendessen“, but more a light version of dinner, like an evening snack, since Germans traditionally have their main meal at lunchtime. And note the way how she pronounces the word Spaghetti, more like „Spageddi“. You normally wouldn’t say it like that, but it sounds pretty cool (to me).

31) And therefore therefore I need you like a saint, like saint its halo
Krasses Solo, Baby
32) phat solo, baby
The word „klein“ in line 30 is probably used figuratively and therefore doesn’t mean small, but petty or narrow-minded. It’s a bit difficult to explain how the word „krass“ (line 32) is used in colloquial German. It is very common and it is mostly used in an appreciative sense, i.e. nodding and saying „Krasser Typ!“, if someone sees an extraordinary athlete or an anyhow impressive person.
