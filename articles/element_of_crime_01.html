Am Ende denk' ich immer nur an Dich

by Element of Crime

I have a good friend who has been always about two steps ahead of me, when it comes to musical taste. When he first introduced me to Element of Crime, I just didn’t like it. The sound, the lyrics, the style – everything was a bit weird. Only after about two years later, I discovered this band some other way and it took me and my ego quite some time to admit to my friend, that he was so right!

Although my taste has gone through many changes since then, this band stayed. It reminds me of my North German home and it became a solid ritual to listen to my Element of Crime playlist whenever I drive home through the flat, boring and most times rainy landscapes of Northern Germany.

This Band from my hometown Bremen is almost as old as I am. They have been around for about 35 years now, starting in the 80s with some English albums and then turned to German lyrics in early the 90s. They perform beautiful melancholic songs somewhere between rock, pop and chanson music. The bandleader, Sven Regener has a unique voice with a subtle north German accent. The sound is enriched by various instruments, that go beyond a normal band setup and is weirdly contrary to the mostly monotonous singing of Regener. Many songs have extensive instrumental parts and the unusual good solos are so much better than almost everything I've heard.

“Am Ende denk ich immer nur an Dich“ is one of their newer songs from the 2009-released album “Immer da wo du bist bin ich nie” (Wherever you are, I’m not). The lyrics are of course from Sven Regener himself and a perfect example for the poetic and musical style of this great band!

There is a really cool live recording of this song from a TV-Show called “Inas Nacht” (Ina’s night), which is set up in a really small and typical North German pub. And don’t worry, Regener’s scary motionless stare in this performance is just an act that suits the song perfectly. He seems to be a cool guy otherwise. ;-)

Lyrics

Auf einem Spielplatz ruft ein Kind nach seiner Mutter,
1 On a playground, a child calls for his mother,
Damit die sieht, wie hoch das Kind schon schaukeln kann,
2 So that she can see how high the child can swing,
Und es wirft die Beine vor und hoch zum Himmel,
3 And he throws his legs up high to the sky
Bis ein Schuh davonfliegt, und der landet dann
4 Until a shoe flies away, and then it lands

This fourth line alone makes no sense, but it will be completed in the next verse (line 5). In written language, this is nothing special, but if you listen to the song you can hear that the emphasis on last words in that line suggests, that the sentence ends here. In German language it is even more noticable, because unlike the translation, the line ends on “dann” (= then) and it seems like the sentence is kind of hanging in the air. This stylistic trick is very typical for Element of Crime. Sometimes, like in the song “Mittelpunkt der Welt” (an article on that one might follow), they use it so confusingly, that the whole meaning stays vague.

Auf einem Auto, das am Straßenrand geparkt ist,
5 On a car that’s parked on the side of the road
Auf dessen Windschutzscheibe "Schwein" geschrieben steht,
6 on its windshild was written „pig“
Und das metallic-braun und glatt wie Deine Haare
7 and that metallic brown and smooth like your hair
Genau wie Du sein wahres Alter nicht verrät.
8 just like you doesn't reveal its real age.
Chorus:
Chorus:
Ganz egal, woran ich grade denke,
9 It doesn’t matter at all, what I'm thinking
Am Ende denk' ich immer nur an Dich.
10 In the end I only think of you.

The first eight lines only make sense after this chorus. The song begins with a series of loosely related observations about a child and a car. From there it leads into a weird and unexpected association about his lost girlfriend.

Die deutsche Mutter stürmt nach vorn in heller Panik
11 The German mother storms forward in a panic
Und übersieht dabei ein Kindesbein im Sand
12 And overlooks a child's leg in the sand
Und schlägt lang hin, da lacht der Kindesbeinbesitzer,
13 And falls over, so that the child's leg owner laughs,
Der hat ein Erdbeereis in seiner rechten Hand.
14 He has a strawberry ice cream in his right hand.

Why the term “deutsche” mother is used in line 11, is up for interpretation, but I’d suggest, he is referring to typical overprotective helicopter parents. Parents from other cultures are often viewed as more relaxed (or careless) than the typical well off middle or upper class German parents. The words “schlägt lang hin“ in line 13 is a rarely used term for “falling over” but it further more conveys the meaning of not just tumbling, but falling flat and heavily on the ground.
If you have learned some German already, you know you can just connect words to create new words. However, just because you can, doesn’t mean you should do that with all words. “Kindesbeinbesitzer” is grammatically correct and every German will understand the meaning, but nobody would use that word in a normal context. It sounds quite awkward but fits pretty good into the general weirdness of these lyrics.

Das hängt bedenklich schräg nach vorn in seiner Waffel
15 It [the ice cream] hangs worryingly at an angle in its waffle
Und tropft sich selbst verschwendend auf die Haute Couture
16 And drips, wasting itself, on the haute couture
Am Leib des ganzen Stolzes seiner schönen Eltern
17 On the body of the whole pride of his beautiful parents
Und wird zu Dreck dort, genau wie ich bei Dir.
18 And turns to dirt there, just like me for you.

“sich selbst verschwenden” is a term you would normally use for people who waste their life or their talents by not doing the right thing or fulfilling their purpose. “Haute Couture” is just (like in English) the French term for high fashion, but it is rarely used in German. Together with line 17, it further emphasizes the posh character of the parents.

“Der Mensch” (= the human) is a very common way to refer to mankind (or people) in general. The “Tat des Beinestellens” again is a quite awkward use of German language, however the meaning is completely clear to native German speakers.
